The problem

A city’s traffic data is scattered across a dozen screens. Decisions aren’t.

A collision blocks an arterial. The camera saw it, dispatch logged it, buses are already rerouting around it — but each of those lives in a different system, and the operator retiming the corridor works from phone calls and memory. When the after-action review comes, the record of who changed what, when, and why is a spreadsheet, if it exists at all. Nexus City OS puts detection, decision support and the paper trail in one place, without taking the human out of the loop.

How it works

Detect → Recommend → Approve. In that order, every time.

The AI never touches a signal on its own. It drafts; the operator disposes. The workflow below is the only path a change can take.

STEP 01

Detect & triage

Edge vision on live camera frames, correlated 911 dispatches and congestion anomalies raise incidents into a two-tier triage inbox: Critical Action for what needs a decision now, Monitored Anomalies for everything else. Full keyboard control — J/K to move, Enter to open.

STEP 02

Draft a mitigation

The copilot proposes concrete signal-timing operations — which intersection, which phase, how many seconds of green — with a dry-run simulation, its data provenance, and a composite confidence score. Plans that fail safety checks never reach the operator.

STEP 03

Approve, execute, audit

The operator approves or rejects the exact plan content identified by its SHA-256 hash. Execution is mode-dependent (shadow, advisory or live), monitored for worsening conditions, and revertible in one action. Everything writes to an append-only, hash-chained log.

Governance

Deployed on a ladder. Promoted only by an administrator.

Cities don’t adopt automation in one step. Nexus City OS runs the identical detection and recommendation pipeline in three modes — the difference is only what an approval is allowed to do. Mode changes are admin-only and audit-logged, and the platform restarts into the last authorized mode, never silently upward.

Shadow

Log only

Approved plans are recorded, never executed. Run it for a season, then compare what the platform would have done against what your operators did. This is where every deployment starts.

Advisory

Instruct the field

Approval issues a formatted instruction for signal staff — current timing, requested change, confirmation protocol — that expires unconfirmed after 15 minutes. People still turn the keys.

Live

Execute, monitor, revert

Approved changes apply to signal timing, are watched for worsening congestion in a 5-minute window, and revert in one action — automatic rollback is proposed if conditions degrade ≥ 20%.

A safety gate the AI cannot argue with.

Every drafted plan passes deterministic checks before an operator ever sees it. These are code, not prompts — a plan that fails is blocked and the block itself is logged.

HALLUCINATION Every entity the plan references must exist in the city graph. A recommendation naming a phantom intersection is discarded before review.
MUTCD BOUNDS Timing changes are checked against engineering constraints — minimum green, pedestrian crossing time, yellow-clearance for the approach speed. Out-of-bounds plans are blocked.
PROVENANCE A plan must carry its evidence: the data sources, their timestamps and a written rationale. No provenance, no review.
CONFIDENCE Composite confidence below the governed threshold (admin-set, 50–95%) is withheld rather than shown. The threshold itself is a logged, admin-only control.
CONCURRENCY A hard cap on simultaneous live timing changes keeps the blast radius bounded — new plans are blocked until active changes settle or revert.
AUDIT CHAIN Every governance event — detection, acknowledgment, recommendation, approval, execution, rollback, note, field contact, export — is appended to a SHA-256 hash chain and persisted in SQLite. Tampering breaks the chain visibly; the console verifies it continuously.

Certified, verifiable, refusable — by design.

The safety gate isn't just internal. Every plan the platform drafts leaves an artifact anyone can check, and the same rulepack is exposed as a service — so a third party can verify our work without trusting our word.

Certify

A signature on every plan

Each drafted plan carries an HMAC-signed safety certificate, embedded in the hash chain before the entry is sealed. The certificate is issuer-verifiable; asymmetric public-key signatures are on the roadmap.

Verify

Rulepack as an API

A stateless MUTCD rulepack verification API takes a timing plan and returns per-rule R1–R5 pass/fail — plus a certificate-verify endpoint that confirms any Nexus-issued cert's signature and chain membership. Authenticated, no city graph required.

Refuse

Read-only, and it says no

A read-only MCP endpoint lets an AI assistant query incidents, plans and the audit trail. Ask it to act and it returns a structured safety refusal — no mutation path exists.

The pilot ships a counterfactual Decision Audit — evidence of what the AI would have done in Shadow mode versus what your operators did — and a NIST AI RMF critical-infrastructure conformity pack. Community Watch

A thousand extra eyes — every one of them verified.

Residents sign up in seconds, set a custom alert radius (100 m to 10 km), and get notified the moment a collision, fire response or 911 dispatch is detected near them. Then they help: a photo taken at the scene is checked by AI vision before anything is published — verified confirmations attach to the actual incident record the city operator is working, never a separate unvetted post.

STEP 01

Get alerted nearby

Citizens receive browser notifications for incidents inside their chosen radius — collisions, stopped vehicles, pedestrian hazards — with live distance, confirmation counts and a community comment thread on every incident.

STEP 02

Confirm with a photo

One tap opens the camera. The photo runs through the AI vision gate: if the model sees a real incident, the confirmation is appended to the incident's action history and the hash-chained audit trail. A proximity check (750 m) stops armchair confirmations.

STEP 03

Report what the cameras missed

Vision-verified citizen reports become real incidents in the operator queue, with the citizen photo as the frozen evidence frame. Duplicates auto-attach as corroboration. When AI can't verify, a human operator moderates — unverified content is never auto-published.

Trust is engineered, not assumed: per-user rate limits, prompt-injection guards on all community text, a reputation ledger that rewards verified evidence, and citizen accounts that are hard-gated off every operator control — a citizen can see and contribute, never actuate.

Questions cities ask

The short answers.

Does the AI ever change a signal by itself?
No. There is no code path that executes a physical change without an operator approving a specific, hash-identified plan — and in Shadow and Advisory modes, not even approval touches hardware. The approval requirement is a constant in the plan model, not a setting.
What happens when a change makes things worse?
Executed changes are monitored for five minutes against their pre-change baseline. If congestion worsens ≥ 20%, the platform alerts and proposes reversion; the operator can revert any executed plan in one action at any time. Reversion restores the exact previous timing, which is stored with the plan.
Can the audit log be edited after the fact?
Each entry includes the SHA-256 hash of the previous entry. Editing or deleting any record breaks the chain, and the console header verifies chain integrity continuously. The full log exports as JSONL for public-records requests — and the export itself is logged.
What does it take to run?
One process. Python standard library, SQLite on local disk, a Leaflet map in the browser. No external database, broker or orchestration. It has been run on a laptop; a TOC workstation is ample. Restart recovery is built in: operating mode, incidents, plans and the audit chain persist.
How does role-based access work?
Four roles — viewer, analyst, operator, admin — enforced on every privileged action, with denials logged. Operators acknowledge and approve; only admins change the operating mode or the confidence threshold. Sessions are signed tokens; single sign-on via Cloudflare Access is supported for zero-trust deployments.
What about resident privacy?
Telemetry is redacted at the edge and unredacted payloads are rejected at ingestion — that rejection is itself audit-logged. Camera frames are used for incident detection and kept as detection-time evidence tied to specific incidents, then released from memory on a retention schedule. There is no face or plate recognition anywhere in the pipeline.
Can a third party verify a plan without trusting you?
Yes. Every drafted plan carries an HMAC-signed certificate embedded in the hash chain, and a stateless verification API returns per-rule MUTCD R1–R5 results for any submitted timing plan plus a certificate-verify endpoint that confirms a cert's signature and chain membership. The signatures are issuer-verifiable (symmetric HMAC) today; asymmetric public-key signatures are on the roadmap, and we say so plainly.
What can an AI assistant actually do through the platform?
The MCP endpoint is read-only. An assistant can query incidents, plans and the audit trail (subject to role), but any attempt to act returns a structured safety refusal — there is no mutation path behind it. This is the honest core of the product today: verification, provenance and abstention. Verified AI actuation is a roadmap slide, and a read-only NTCIP 1202 controller bridge is its credible first step — not something the pilot executes.
Our signals aren’t in Seattle. How does onboarding work?
Cities plug in through an adapter that maps local feeds (cameras, transit, dispatch, weather) onto the platform’s city graph. The Tacoma deployment is the working example: same engine, different adapter. Start in Shadow mode against your real feeds, measure for a season, then decide whether Advisory earns its place.
